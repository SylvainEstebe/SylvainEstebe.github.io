## But — does this already exist?

Yes. In Texas.

### K-9 Cinemas, Plano, Texas

K-9 Cinemas opened in Plano in 2018, built from scratch to welcome dogs alongside their humans. No rows of seats — just couches. The ticket included two beers, and the atmosphere was apparently closer to someone's living room than a movie theatre[^k9].

It didn't last as a business — a few years, then it folded. But the idea keeps coming back: *Pup Up Cinema* in London, *Sit Stay Cinema* across several British cities, occasional dog-friendly screenings in independent venues in France and the US.

## A short history of dogs at the cinema

The idea of bringing a dog *into* the theatre is recent. The idea of putting one *in* the film is much older.

### As stars

Rin Tin Tin shot his first features in Hollywood in 1922 — a German shepherd brought back from a French battlefield in 1918, who ended up making Warner Brothers enough money to save them from bankruptcy. Lassie followed in the 1940s. Uggie, the Jack Russell from *The Artist* (2011), was given the Palm Dog at Cannes the same year — a real ceremony, on the red carpet.

The dog-as-actor has always worked. Probably because dogs don't act: they look, they wait, they react. We project our own moods onto them, which is exactly the thing they're best at receiving.

### As audience

The idea that a dog could *be in the room* is much more recent — the 2010s, more or less, riding the wave of *pet-parenting* culture. Before that the idea simply didn't surface — it hadn't occurred to anyone that they were the ones we should be thinking of.

### And what it would actually do to them

A dog's hearing reaches up to 45 kHz, against our 20. A modern blockbuster peaks around 100 dB, sometimes more. Multiplying one by the other gives a good sense of why: for Ada, a Marvel screening on a Saturday night probably isn't the treat we'd like to imagine.

A quiet cinema, an older film, gentle projection, a couch — like Plano — that's a different thing. There she might actually fall asleep at my feet.
